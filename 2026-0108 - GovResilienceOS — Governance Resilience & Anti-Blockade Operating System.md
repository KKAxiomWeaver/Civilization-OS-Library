## 02 â€” Design Objectives  

Without changing constitutional structures or legislative powers, GovResilienceOS aims to:

1. **Reduce Single-Point Blockade Risk**  
   - No single budget package or procedure should be able to paralyze an entire functional domain.  

2. **Distribute Blockade Costs**  
   - Negative consequences of blockade should surface in **multi-level, multi-constituency, and multi-departmental** metrics, not only on central executors.  

3. **Maintain Policy Accumulation**  
   - Use multiple branches, small increments, and hidden confluence so resilience-building can **continue gradually** under political contention.  

4. **Increase Transparency & Public Understanding**  
   - Make concrete delays observable and traceable to institutional bottlenecks, enabling more informed public debate and institutional adjustment.  

GovResilienceOS treats these as **OS-level properties**, not ad-hoc political tactics.

## 03 â€” Six-Axis Governance Grid  

To enable branch refinement and modular design, GovResilienceOS uses a **six-axis grid** as its base coordinate system.  

### 3.1 Legal Obligation Axis  

Includes:

- Salaries for civil servants, military, police, and firefighters;  
- Basic traffic maintenance (signals, essential surface upkeep);  
- Garbage collection and baseline sanitation;  
- Minimum water & power operations and minimum disaster-response capacity.  

These â€œä¸å¯ä¸­æ–·â€ expenditures form a **stable base** less likely to be targeted; they are prime candidates for embedding **resilience side-branches**.  

### 3.2 Cross-Ministry Administrative Axis  

Includes:

- Cabinet-level coordination;  
- Routine inspection, planning integration, data sharing, and joint planning.  

Much of this can continue even under resource constraints; it anchors **design and planning continuity** that does not directly hinge on new budgets.  

### 3.3 Disaster & Resilience Axis  

Includes:

- Typhoon, earthquake, and other natural-disaster prevention and response;  
- Backup and recovery for energy, communications, transport, and logistics;  
- Maintenance and upgrading of shelter conditions.  

This axis has **high social legitimacy** and overlaps strongly with defense and logistics; it can be protected via special funds and emergency procedures.  

### 3.4 Local Autonomy Axis  

Includes:

- Road patching, drainage improvements, community works, school-building strengthening;  
- Direct maintenance of local public facilities by counties, cities, and townships.  

Blocking this axis **directly hits local performance and daily life** of voters, raising political costs for blockers.  

### 3.5 SOE Surplus Axis  

Includes:

- Internal investments and O&M plans by major SOEs (power, water, rail, etc.);  
- Projects that do not fully rely on general fiscal outlays.  

This axis can host **resilience and critical equipment upgrades** without increasing headline budgets.  

### 3.6 Special Budget Axis  

Includes:

- Defense procurement specials;  
- Multi-year major infrastructure, transport networks, energy transition plans.  

Once passed, they enjoy multi-year execution windows and are relatively shielded from **annual** blockade patterns.

## 04 â€” Core Design I: Branch Refinement  

### 4.1 Concept  

**Branch refinement** means:

> Decomposing functions that used to be bundled in a few large projects / single budget packages  
> into many smaller, moderate-sized items distributed across **different axes and levels**.  

Goals:

- Reduce the immediate system-wide impact of any single blockade;  
- Ensure blockadeâ€™s negative effects **touch multiple levels and constituencies**, creating counterbalancing pressure.  

### 4.2 Operational Principles  

- **Smaller case sizes, same total functionality**  
  - Replace mega-projects with more, smaller batches of works.  

- **Cross-axis distribution**  
  - For a given function (e.g., flood control, shelter upgrade), split tasks into:  
    - Local autonomy axis,  
    - SOE axis,  
    - Disaster & resilience axis, etc.  

- **High-salience sub-items**  
  - Prioritize items with high daily-life impact:  
    - Specific roads, drains, shelters, medical backup power, etc.  

### 4.3 Structural Shift in Blockade Dynamics  

Under legacy structure:

- Block one large budget â†’ many sub-projects are quietly delayed;  
- Public anger defaults to â€œæ”¿åºœæ²’åš / åŸ·è¡Œä¸åŠ›â€ narratives.  

Under branch refinement:

- Blocking a package affects **many visible local projects**;  
- Local governments and constituents experience delay directly;  
- Blockade cost shifts from **one-way** (central exec only) to **multi-way**, raising decision thresholds.

## 05 â€” Core Design II: Multi-Module Governance  

### 5.1 Multi-Module Sharing  

For any **national critical function**, e.g.:

- Non-interruptible medical service under disaster;  
- Logistics backbone usable both in peace and war;  
- Baseline resilience of energy, communications, and transport.  

Decompose into modules assigned to different ministries and levels:

- Medical module â†’ health and medical system;  
- Transport module â†’ MOTC and local public-works;  
- Energy module â†’ economics / energy agencies;  
- Communications module â†’ telecom regulators and tech agencies;  
- Logistics module â†’ defense and relevant support units.  

Effect:

- Blocking a single ministryâ€™s budget cannot fully paralyze the function;  
- Execution inherently pushes toward **cross-ministry coordination and integration**.  

### 5.2 Impact on Blockade Strategies  

Under multi-module governance, blockade entails:

- Accepting responsibility for delays in **multi-ministry projects**;  
- Bearing the displeasure of multiple local governments;  
- Elevating risk for multiple classes of critical facilities (political cost).  

Blockade cost rises from **localized** to **system-wide**, encouraging a return to **case-by-case scrutiny** rather than blanket denial.

## 06 â€” Core Design III: Hidden Confluence  

### 6.1 Avoid Single Terminal Nodes  

Visible, flagship confluence (one big project) is:

- Good for publicity;  
- But in polarized environments, becomes a **single high-value blockade target**.  

**Hidden confluence** accepts that:

- Results may be **presented in a distributed way**;  
- Not all effects must converge into one project name or one completion date;  
- Overall resilience is evaluated via **indicators and cumulative outcomes**, not one ribbon-cutting.  

### 6.2 Practical Expression  

Examples of system-level indicators:

- Annual grid outage time;  
- Disaster recovery time for key systems;  
- Flood incident counts and severity;  
- Shelter capacity, coverage, and equipment readiness;  
- Road and bridge safety metrics across years.  

Blockers can no longer easily â€œstop resilienceâ€ by vetoing a single flagship project;  
they would have to **systemically erode many small items**, which is both harder and more visible.
